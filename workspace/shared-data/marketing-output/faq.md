# DPDP Act 2023 — Frequently Asked Questions

> Everything your business needs to know about India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act.

---

## General

### Q1: What is the DPDP Act?
The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 is India's comprehensive data protection law. It governs how organisations collect, process, store, and share digital personal data of individuals in India. The Act was signed into law on 11 August 2023, and the detailed DPDP Rules were published on 13 November 2025.

### Q2: When does it come into effect?
Key dates:
- **13 November 2025** — Rules 1, 2, and 17-21 (definitions, appointments, digital office procedures)
- **13 November 2026** — Rule 4 (Consent Manager registration)
- **13 May 2027** — Majority of substantive compliance rules
- The Act's provisions themselves will be notified by the Central Government on a date to be announced.

### Q3: Who needs to comply?
Any organisation that processes digital personal data is affected:
- Companies based in India collecting data from individuals in India
- Companies outside India offering goods or services to individuals in India
- Government agencies and State instrumentalities (with some exemptions)
- Data Processors (cloud providers, SaaS vendors, etc.)

### Q4: Does this apply to my small business?
Yes — there are no size-based exemptions for general obligations. However, the Central Government may exempt certain Data Fiduciaries for up to 5 years (Section 17(5)). The additional obligations for Significant Data Fiduciaries (DPO, DPIA, audit) will apply only to notified entities.

## Consent & Notice

### Q5: What makes consent valid under the DPDP Act?
Consent must be:
- **Free** — not coerced
- **Specific** — tied to a particular purpose
- **Informed** — Data Principal knows what they're consenting to
- **Unconditional** — not tied to unrelated conditions
- **Unambiguous** — clear intent
- **Affirmative action** — opt-in required (no pre-ticked boxes)

### Q6: Can I use pre-ticked consent boxes?
No. Consent requires a clear affirmative action. Passive consent (pre-ticked boxes, implied consent by continuing use) does not meet the standard.

### Q7: What information must my privacy notice include?
Your notice must state:
- What personal data is being collected (itemised)
- The specific purpose for processing
- How to exercise rights and withdraw consent
- How to complain to the Data Protection Board
- DPO or contact person details
- Available in English or Eighth Schedule language (upon request)

### Q8: Can a Data Principal withdraw consent?
Yes — and it must be as easy to withdraw consent as it was to give it. Upon withdrawal, you must cease processing within a reasonable time, unless retention is required or authorised by law.

### Q9: How long do I have to respond to a consent withdrawal?
The Act says "within a reasonable time." This is not precisely defined, so we recommend setting an internal policy (e.g., 30 days) and documenting your rationale.

## Children's Data

### Q10: What counts as a "child"?
An individual who has not completed the age of 18 years.

### Q11: What do I need to do before processing a child's data?
You must obtain **verifiable consent of the parent or lawful guardian**. This requires due diligence to verify the parent's identity and age through authorised entities (e.g., DigiLocker, Aadhaar-based verification per Rule 10).

### Q12: What is prohibited for children's data?
- Tracking or behavioural monitoring of children
- Targeted advertising directed at children
- Any processing likely to cause detrimental effect on a child's well-being

### Q13: Are there any exemptions?
Yes — the Fourth Schedule lists classes of Data Fiduciaries and purposes exempt from child data obligations, including:
- Clinical establishments and healthcare professionals providing health services
- Educational institutions
- Mental health establishments
- Certain situations specified by notification

## Security & Breaches

### Q14: What security measures do I need?
At minimum (Rule 6):
- Encryption, obfuscation, or masking of personal data
- Role-based access controls
- Audit logs and monitoring
- Regular backups
- Valid contracts with Data Processors
- Log retention for at least one year

### Q15: What happens if there's a data breach?
1. **Immediately** — Intimate the Data Protection Board (initial notification)
2. **Immediately** — Intimate each affected Data Principal
3. **Within 72 hours** — Submit a detailed report to the Board with description, consequences, measures taken, and contact information

### Q16: What are the penalties for failing to report a breach?
Up to ₹200 crore for breach notification failures.

## Data Subject Rights

### Q17: What rights do individuals have under the DPDP Act?
- **Right to information** — know what data is collected and why
- **Right to correction** — correct inaccurate or misleading data
- **Right to erasure** — request deletion of personal data
- **Right to grievance redressal** — have complaints addressed
- **Right to nominate** — nominate someone to exercise rights after death/incapacity

### Q18: How quickly must I respond to a grievance?
Within **90 days** of receiving the grievance (Rule 14(3)).

## Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs)

### Q19: What is a Significant Data Fiduciary?
An SDF is a Data Fiduciary notified by the Central Government under Section 10, typically based on:
- Volume and sensitivity of personal data processed
- Risk to rights of Data Principals
- Turnover or revenue
- Any other criteria specified

### Q20: What additional obligations do SDFs have?
- Appoint a **Data Protection Officer** (based in India)
- Appoint an **independent data auditor**
- Conduct **annual DPIA and audit**
- Report DPIA/audit to the Board
- Algorithmic software due diligence
- Data localisation for specified data

---

## Data Transfers & Localisation

### Q21: Can I transfer data outside India?
Yes, to countries not restricted by the Central Government. The government may notify certain territories where transfers are restricted. SDFs may have additional localisation requirements for specified personal data.

### Q22: Is there a requirement to store data in India?
Not generally, but the government may specify certain personal data that must be stored locally. SDFs may face additional localisation obligations.

## Penalties & Enforcement

### Q23: What's the maximum penalty?
Up to **₹250 crore** for failure to implement security safeguards. See the full penalty schedule:

| Violation | Max Penalty |
|-----------|-------------|
| Security safeguard failures | ₹250 crore |
| Breach notification failures | ₹200 crore |
| Children's data violations | ₹200 crore |
| SDF obligation violations | ₹150 crore |
| General non-compliance | ₹50 crore |
| Data Principal duty violations | ₹10,000 |

### Q24: Who enforces the Act?
The **Data Protection Board of India** — an independent body established under Section 18. The Board has powers to inquire into breaches, impose penalties, issue directions, and conduct digital proceedings.

### Q25: Can I appeal a Board decision?
Yes — appeals go to the **Appellate Tribunal** (TDSAT) within 60 days of receiving the order. The Tribunal shall endeavour to dispose of appeals within 6 months.

## Compliance Readiness

### Q26: What should I do first?
1. Map your data flows — what personal data do you collect, why, and where?
2. Review your consent mechanisms — are they compliant?
3. Assess security safeguards — encryption, access controls, logging?
4. Draft breach notification procedures
5. Establish grievance redressal (90-day capability)
6. Audit Data Processor contracts

### Q27: Do I need a Data Protection Officer?
Only if you are notified as a Significant Data Fiduciary. However, you must publish contact information of someone who can answer questions about data processing (Rule 9).

### Q28: What if I'm not ready by the compliance deadline?
The Rules come into force on a staggered basis (some at 1 year, most at 18 months from November 2025). Use this time actively — map, assess, implement. The Board may consider good-faith efforts in penalty determination.

# India's DPDP Act Is Here: Your 6-Month Compliance Playbook

*The DPDP Rules are out. Here's what your business needs to do now.*

---

**13 November 2025** — that's the day India's data protection framework went from "coming soon" to "clock is ticking." The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology published the **Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025**, turning the 2023 Act into actionable compliance requirements.

If you run a business that handles personal data in India — whether you're a startup, a mid-size company, or a global enterprise — here's your playbook.

---

## The Timeline You Need to Know

The Rules come into force on a staggered schedule:

| Date | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **Already in force** | Definitions, Board appointments, digital office procedures |
| **13 November 2026** | Consent Manager registration rules |
| **13 May 2027** | **Everything else** — notices, security, breach reporting, erasure, verifiable consent |

That's **18 months** from publication for the big stuff. It sounds like a lot of time, but for most organisations it's just enough to do this properly.

---

## What the DPDP Act Actually Means

At its core, the DPDP Act is about three things:

1. **Putting individuals in control** of their personal data
2. **Making organisations accountable** for how they handle that data
3. **Creating a robust enforcement mechanism** through the Data Protection Board of India

The Act covers digital personal data processed within India, and also applies to processing outside India if it's in connection with offering goods or services to people in India.

**Translation:** If you have Indian customers, this applies to you.

## The 5 Things You Need to Do Right Now

### 1. Map Everything

You cannot comply with a law you don't understand. Start with a complete data map:

- What personal data do you collect?
- Where does it live?
- Who has access to it?
- Who do you share it with (vendors, partners, cloud providers)?
- How long do you keep it?

**Pro tip:** Most breaches come from data you forgot you had. A thorough data map is the single most valuable thing you can do.

### 2. Fix Your Consent Flows

Under the DPDP Act, consent must be:
- **Free** — no coercion
- **Specific** — purpose must be clearly stated
- **Informed** — Data Principal knows what they're agreeing to
- **Unambiguous** — clear affirmative action (no pre-ticked boxes)
- **Withdrawable** — as easy to withdraw as it was to give

If you're currently using pre-ticked boxes or bundled consent (where agreeing to one thing means agreeing to everything), you need to change that.

**The litmus test:** Can a user withdraw consent in the same number of clicks it took them to give it? If not, you're not compliant.

### 3. Lock Down Security

Rule 6 of the DPDP Rules spells out the minimum security safeguards:
- Encryption (at rest and in transit)
- Access controls (role-based, least privilege)
- Monitoring and audit logging
- Regular backups
- Valid contracts with every Data Processor

Logs must be retained for at least one year.

### 4. Build a Breach Response Machine

When a breach happens (and it's a matter of when, not if), the clock starts immediately:

| Timeline | Action |
|----------|--------|
| **Immediately** | Notify the Board (initial) |
| **Immediately** | Notify each affected Data Principal |
| **Within 72 hours** | Submit detailed report to the Board |

The penalty for failing to report a breach? Up to **₹200 crore**.

This isn't something you want to figure out the night of a breach. Build your incident response plan now, run tabletop exercises, and make sure everyone knows their role.

### 5. Prepare for Children's Data

If your service is accessible to children (under 18), this is your biggest exposure:
- You need **verifiable parental consent** — identity and age verification through authorised entities
- **No tracking** or behavioural monitoring of children
- **No targeted advertising** to children
- Penalties up to **₹200 crore** for violations

**Good news:** Healthcare providers, educational institutions, and certain other entities have exemptions under the Fourth Schedule.

## What's Different From GDPR?

If you're already GDPR-compliant, you're ahead of the game — but there are important differences:

| Area | GDPR | DPDP Act |
|------|------|----------|
| **Age of consent** | 16 (varies by EU member state) | 18 (child = under 18) |
| **Data portability** | Explicit right | Not explicitly provided |
| **Data Protection Officer** | Mandatory for certain entities | Mandatory only for SDFs |
| **Cross-border transfers** | Adequacy decisions, SCCs, BCRs | Restricted territories to be notified |
| **Fines** | Up to 4% of global turnover | Fixed rupee caps (₹250 crore max) |

## The Penalty Landscape

The Act's penalty schedule is designed to make non-compliance expensive:

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Failure to secure data | ₹250 crore |
| Breach notification failures | ₹200 crore |
| Children's data violations | ₹200 crore |
| SDF obligations violations | ₹150 crore |
| General non-compliance | ₹50 crore |

The Board considers: nature, gravity, duration, type of data, whether it's repetitive, mitigation efforts, and impact on affected persons.

## What About Significant Data Fiduciaries?

If your organisation is large or processes sensitive data at scale, you may be notified as a **Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF)**. SDFs have additional obligations:

- Appoint a **Data Protection Officer** (based in India)
- Appoint an **independent data auditor**
- Conduct **annual DPIA and audit**
- Report findings to the Board
- Perform algorithmic due diligence

If you're a large Indian tech company, e-commerce platform, financial services firm, or social media intermediary — start preparing for SDF status now, even before the notification.

## Your 90-Day Sprint Plan

**Month 1: Discovery**
- Complete data mapping
- Identify gaps in current practices
- Assign ownership for each compliance area

**Month 2: Policy & Process**
- Draft consent notices and privacy policies
- Implement security safeguards
- Build breach response procedures
- Establish grievance redressal mechanism

**Month 3: Implementation**
- Deploy consent management flows
- Test breach notification procedures
- Train staff on new policies
- Audit Data Processor contracts
- Conduct first internal audit

---

## The Bottom Line

The DPDP Act is a significant shift in India's data protection landscape. But with 18 months before the major rules kick in, businesses have a real opportunity to build compliant, privacy-respecting systems from the ground up.

**Don't wait for May 2027.** Start your data map this week. Fix your consent flows this month. Build your breach response this quarter.

The businesses that treat this as an opportunity — to build trust with their customers, strengthen their security posture, and differentiate themselves in the market — will be the ones that come out ahead.
